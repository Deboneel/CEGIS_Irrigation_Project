District-level thematic mapping · Rabi 2023–24

Bangladesh irrigates 72% of its cultivated land. The headline hides a fracture this report maps district by district.

Rain carries the Aman season. The dry Rabi season — November to March — runs almost entirely on pumped water, and how well each district manages that supply decides national food security. Five maps trace where that water comes from, what powers it, and how much of the aquifer is left.

Why this matters

A national average hides who has water, who pays more to pump it, and who's drawing the aquifer down to get it.

A 72% national irrigation rate says almost nothing about sustainability, access, cost, or which regions are quietly being left behind. The district-level spatial view answers those questions in a way a single table cannot. This report maps five variables — irrigation coverage, surface water reliance, groundwater reliance, power source, and groundwater depth — across all 64 districts of Bangladesh.

A geography story Where rivers are slow, wide and seasonally full — the south and northeast — surface irrigation works well. Where groundwater is shallow and reliable — the northwest — it was abandoned in favour of tube wells decades ago. 1,617K ha sounds large but is only 28% of total irrigated area: groundwater dominates nationally.

  • The "Very Large" class (130,000–254,178 ha) — 9 districts — covers 40.3% of all groundwater irrigation; the bottom 9 districts contribute just 0.1%.
  • Northwest concentration is structural: shallow water table (3–7 m), flat alluvial plain, decades of STW/DTW investment make extraction cheap and reliable.
  • Southern districts (Barisal, Khulna coast) show near-zero groundwater use — saline intrusion makes extracted water unsuitable for crops.
  • Hill tracts are low for a different reason — hard-rock aquifers limit yield and terrain blocks tube-well deployment.
The system's load-bearing layer At 4,161K ha, groundwater carries 72% of Bangladesh's total irrigated area. That dependence is geographically uneven — the northwest is built on it, the south has almost none — and it sets up the central risk this report tracks: the same districts showing 99% irrigation rates are drawing the aquifer down every year. That makes groundwater zoning the most critical layer in the whole report.

  • Rajshahi leads electricity adoption at 64.2% — mature rural electrification and decades of STW/DTW grid connections.

An economic liability, not a technical one Diesel dominance in two-thirds of districts costs farmers more per hectare and emits more carbon than electric pumping. Rajshahi's electric share proves the transition is achievable with grid investment — the south and Sylhet lag because infrastructure hasn't followed, not because electricity is unavailable nationally.

  • The 8 critical upazilas average 31.45 m — early warning signals, not yet a crisis, but trending that way.

This is current depletion — not a future risk.

At 4,161K ha, groundwater carries 72% of national irrigation, and the northwest's near-complete coverage was built almost entirely on it. Cross-referencing Layer 03 against Layer 05 confirms the same districts pumping hardest — Rajshahi, parts of Mymensingh and Dhaka division — already sit in moderate-to-deep zoning territory as divisional averages, meaning individual hotspot upazilas run considerably worse.

Eight upazilas already average over 20 m depth, with a peak average of 31.45 m. Without extraction limits or recharge intervention, the northwest's 99% irrigation rate becomes its own biggest vulnerability — the resource it depends on most is the one it's drawing down fastest.

Conclusion

The system works — but its foundation is narrower than the 72% headline suggests.

Bangladesh irrigates 72% of its net cultivated land during Rabi season — an achievement built almost entirely on groundwater in the northwest, where Rajshahi, Rangpur and Mymensingh carry the system. The south sits at the opposite end, limited by salinity and terrain that tube wells cannot fix. Diesel still powers 53.5% of irrigated area, costing farmers more per hectare, while Rajshahi's 64% electric share proves the transition is achievable with grid investment. Three gaps need attention next:

Surface water

2.25 M non-irrigated hectares, concentrated south

Salinity and tidal influence rule out tube wells across the coastal belt — surface-water and managed-flooding strategies are the only realistic path to closing this gap.

Groundwater

Regulation in the northwest, before extraction outruns recharge

Eight upazilas already average over 20 m depth. The districts with the highest irrigation success are the same ones drawing the aquifer down annually.

Power source

Diesel-to-electric transition, south and Sylhet

67% of districts remain diesel-dominant. Rajshahi shows what grid investment can do; closing this gap directly cuts per-hectare costs for smallholder farmers.
